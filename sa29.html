Neo-Liberalism: Can history help
us understand it?


194 comments


In this film, we continue our discussion of political concepts, and how they are used by historians. Dean talks to Matthew Francis, a political historian at the University of Birmingham, about the challenges in studying the concept of neoliberalism.


Neoliberalism is a good example of a contemporary political concept that both has power in understanding the recent past, and also presents problems for the researcher. Matthew describes two problems with studying neoliberalism:


-People use this concept to describe different things at the same time. For some, ‘neoliberalism’ describes a particular school of economic and political thought. For others, it is used more broadly, to describe a range of behaviours and ideas prevalent at a particular time.


-Nobody uses the concept to identify themselves. Neoliberalism is used in a pejorative way, and even those described as being leaders of a neoliberal school reject this term.


As we have discussed this week, the meanings of concepts can often be unclear and contested. However, they can still be of value to the researcher. In the case of neoliberalism, this can still be used to define a way of thinking that has shared characteristics. It is useful as it can be used in contrast to ways of thinking that were accepted and influential immediately before and at the same time. Like other contested concepts, ‘neoliberalism’ can also be used by historians to critically assess how far a new concept replaces older ones, or whether much of existing ways of thinking remain dominant. Matthew suggests that, despite accounts saying that neoliberalism replaced earlier ways of thinking about the state and the economy, many of the features of those earlier ideologies remain in place.


Why not use the comment thread below this learning step to consider some of these issues in relation to your own research interests. What are the important political words or concepts for your research theme? Are they “contested”, i.e. do different actors use them in different ways? How might reflecting on such differences shape the way you pose your research questions?
