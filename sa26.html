The historical baggage of political language


302 comments


Political concepts have a powerful impact on the way that we perceive events in the world, and express the things that are most important to us. The language that we use to define these concepts is central to the way that we think about them. In this film, Dean talks to David Civil, who researches political use of language, about the power of concepts and the problems with studying political language.


Dean and David express the significance of understanding concepts in the study of history and the power of concepts to shape the way that we view the world. Examples of concepts are, ‘freedom’, ‘security’, or ‘rights’.


However, concepts don’t have fixed meanings. They change with time, and often people don’t agree on their meanings or interpretations. Different people or groups will try to control the way a concept is understood by others. Another way of saying this is to describe concepts as ‘contested’.


The historian faces challenges in studying the use of concepts.


-The meanings of concepts, and the meaning of language used to describe concepts changes over time and between groups. Sometimes this meaning change can be identified by looking at other words used alongside a concept.


-Concepts are contested. That means that different groups or people will try to control an interpretation, and will not necessarily reflect other interpretations in their writings.


-A historian will need to look at the wider historical context within which a group was describing a concept in order to decode the meaning of the language that they use.


In this film, Dean asks David for an example of a concept that has changed meaning over time. David gives ‘meritocracy’ as an example. For the discussion in this step, can you give examples of other concepts that have changed over time?
