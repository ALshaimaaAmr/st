Avoiding being asked



230 comments



So far we have explored who gives, why they might be motivated to do so, and how they may choose which causes to give to. Understanding these motivations will be key to helping you identify your potential supporters. However, there is also strong evidence – both from the academic research and from the experiences of fundraisers – that almost all donations to charity are prompted in some way.



Indeed, as we noted previously, Bekkers (2005) finds that 85% of all donations are given in response to a direct ask. Many fundraisers themselves operate on the maxim “if you don’t ask you don’t get” (Gunstone & Ellison, 2017). Further research from philanthropy scholars find that asking is one of the key drivers of giving to charity (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2007). So much so that one economics researcher refers to asking as the “iron law” of fundraising (Andreoni, 2004).



But all the research mentioned above also suggests that giving decisions are far more complex that just responding to an ask for a donation. Indeed, further research by James Andreoni and his colleagues, provides evidence that some people may even avoid being asked (Andreoni et al, 2011) because the request of a gift can be such a strong trigger of other giving motivations, such as empathy, guilt or (as we will investigate later in this course) obligation. This makes the decision about whether or not to give uncomfortable.



Luke not only learns from the wise master Obi-Wan Kenobi and the earthy Han Solo, but also learns to draw on his own inner strength and resources. He learns to trust ‘the Force’.



This is what psychologists would call developing a sense of ‘self-efficacy’: a confidence in your ability to complete the task at hand. This is a complex skill to learn, but it can be learnt and it’s crucial to developing resilience.



Throughout the rest of this course, we hope to share techniques and resources to assist with this process. You may wish to revisit your comments here to track how your opinions may have changed as you become more confident about your own fundraising.


Further Reading (optional task)



You can access and read Andreoni et al’s (2011) full report on avoiding being asked, if you have the time. The researchers raise some interesting insights into how individuals especially might respond to some fundraising approaches that you might find useful.
